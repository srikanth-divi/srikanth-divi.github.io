Projects

  • sim-codes
    Code repository related to simulation methods including molecular dynamics, accelerated molecular dynamics, and Monte Carlo-based workflows.
  • Nano-thermodynamic model for multi-metal alloy nanoparticles
    Developed a model to predict species distribution at facets, edges, vertices, and bulk regions, with an important size-independent interpretation.
  • Tunable mixing rules for EAM potentials
    Improved embedded-atom method potentials for binary and ternary alloys using heats of mixing to obtain more realistic thermodynamic behavior.
  • Temperature-Programmed Molecular Dynamics (TPMD)
    Developed an accelerated approach for rare-event sampling, with reported large speed-up over conventional molecular dynamics for selected systems.
  • Silicon thin film growth simulations
    Used Parallel Replica Molecular Dynamics to study atomic processes such as hop, exchange, diffusion, and concerted atom movement during film growth.
  • Molecular simulation applications
    Research applications include asphaltene aggregation inhibition, drug encapsulation, nanoemulsions, lithium extraction, and deep eutectic solvent systems.

Research

Dr. Divi's research focuses on computational chemical engineering, especially molecular simulations of materials and chemical systems relevant to catalysis, nanoparticles, interfaces, and advanced formulations.

Research Areas

  • Molecular dynamics and Monte Carlo simulations.
  • Alloy nanoparticles, segregation, and nano-thermodynamics.
  • Catalytic surface reactions and kinetic Monte Carlo methods.
  • Energy materials, lithium extraction, and interfacial systems.
  • Drug delivery, polymer carriers, and nanoemulsions.

Research Highlights

  • Modeled species segregation in AuPt, NiPt, and AgAu bimetallic nanoparticles.
  • Designed improved EAM formulations for alloy thermodynamics.
  • Developed accelerated rare-event methods such as TPMD.
  • Worked on pattern-recognition acceleration in Zacros KMC workflows.
  • Studied morphology evolution during silicon thin film growth.
